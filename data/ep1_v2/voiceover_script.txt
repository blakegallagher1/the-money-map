That is the U.S. personal savings rate... now down 11.76 percent from a year ago.

If Americans are still spending, why is the personal savings rate sitting at an uncomfortable 4.5 percent? In this episode, we follow the money stack underneath the consumer... income, savings, and credit... to see whether spending strength is real, or just getting financed differently.

Right now, the Personal Savings Rate stands at 4.5 percent, as of January 2026. One year earlier, it was 5.1 percent. That is a drop of 0.6 percentage points... or 11.76 percent year over year. By recent standards, that is low. Not panic-level by itself... but low enough to tell us the household cushion is getting thinner while the economy still looks solid on the surface.

On the chart, you see a series that can move quietly for months... and then suddenly matter a lot. The savings rate surged during the pandemic period, then normalized, then kept sliding. What stands out now is not one collapse candle. It is the persistence. The line never rebuilt a comfortable buffer. Instead, it drifted lower into 2026. That tells you households have kept absorbing higher costs and steady spending by saving less of each after-tax dollar.

Now zoom out to the rest of the financing stack. Personal Consumption is at 21536.6 billion dollars... up 4.13 percent year over year. So spending is still growing. Total Consumer Credit is 5114679.44 million dollars... up 1.87 percent year over year. Credit is still expanding, even if not explosively. And Median Household Income is 83730.0 dollars... up just 1.26 percent year over year on the latest annual reading. Put those together and the picture gets sharper. Spending is rising faster than income... and savings are falling while credit remains available.

That combination matters because households generally fund consumption from three places... current income, past savings, or borrowed money. Right now, income growth looks modest. Savings are being replenished more slowly. And credit is still filling part of the gap. So the risk is not that consumers suddenly stop tomorrow. The risk is that stress stays hidden longer than markets expect... because families can keep patching the budget before they finally pull back.

Here is what that means in real life. If a household earns 83730.0 dollars a year, a 4.5 percent savings rate means about 3767.85 dollars saved annually... roughly 313.99 dollars a month. At 5.1 percent, that same household would be saving about 4270.23 dollars a year... or 355.85 dollars a month. That is a difference of 502.38 dollars a year. For many families, that is a car insurance bill, part of a rent increase, or a major appliance repair. And if spending keeps rising 4.13 percent while income rises only 1.26 percent... something has to close that gap. Usually that means smaller cash buffers, more card balances, or both. That matters for housing too. A thinner savings cushion means less money for a down payment, less room for closing costs, and less resilience when mortgage, rent, insurance, or property tax bills jump.

So no... a 4.5 percent savings rate does not guarantee recession tomorrow. But it does flash a warning light. Consumers may look steady... while the balance sheet underneath gets weaker.
