0.7 percent.

That is U.S. GDP growth... after 1.9 percent a year earlier.

If growth has cooled this much, why does the economy not feel dramatically worse yet? In this episode, we break down a slowdown without collapse... where output is losing steam, but consumers and the labor market are still sending a more complicated signal.

Right now, GDP Growth Rate stands at 0.7 percent, as of October 2025. One year earlier, it was 1.9 percent. That is a drop of 1.2 percentage points... or 63.16 percent year over year. That is the headline. And it is a sharp loss of momentum. But one weak GDP reading is not automatic proof of recession. What it does tell us is that the economy is expanding much more slowly than it was... and composition now matters a lot.

On the chart, the story is not a clean fall off a cliff. It is a step-down. You see growth running at a healthier pace... then fading lower into this latest 0.7 percent print. The line loses altitude, not all at once, but in a way that keeps narrowing the margin for error. That matters because slowdowns often start this way. Not with one dramatic crash frame... but with repeated deceleration, where each new reading says the engine is still on... just producing less forward thrust.

Now look at the supporting data. Personal Consumption is 21536.6 billion dollars... up 4.13 percent year over year. So households are still spending. The Unemployment Rate is 4.4 percent... up 4.76 percent year over year. That means the job market has softened, but it is not signaling mass labor stress. And Consumer Sentiment is 56.6... up 8.43 percent year over year. That is an improvement, but still a subdued level historically. Put those together and you get a mixed economy. Spending is still alive. Jobs are weaker at the margin. Confidence is recovering... but from a low base.

This is where the story gets interesting. GDP measures output. Consumption measures what households are buying. Unemployment measures labor slack. Sentiment captures how people feel before they change behavior. Those series do not move in perfect lockstep... and they do not turn on the same day. So weak GDP alongside still-growing consumption and only moderate labor softening can happen during a transition period... when the economy is slowing underneath, even before households fully respond.

Here is the real-world version. If GDP growth slows from 1.9 percent to 0.7 percent, businesses usually become more selective before families feel the full hit. That can mean fewer job openings, smaller raises, slower bonus pools, and more caution around hiring. A household spending 4000 dollars a month does not need a recession headline to change behavior. It just needs a rent renewal up 150 dollars, insurance up 90 dollars, and a sense that work feels less secure. That is when people delay a move, skip a renovation, or hold off on buying a first home. And for housing, this kind of slowdown matters because real estate reacts to confidence and income stability... not just the GDP headline itself. We saw versions of this in past soft patches, where growth cooled first... and household decisions followed later.

So no... 0.7 percent GDP growth does not mean the economy has already collapsed. But it does say momentum is thinning out.
